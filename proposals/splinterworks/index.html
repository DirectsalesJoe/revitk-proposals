What We Found

Six findings from the workshop. Ranked by how quickly you'll see results.

Minimal Behaviour Change
1

Estimates break the emotional thread

Your marketing is world-class. The flybys are stunning. Then the estimate arrives as a dry spreadsheet. The emotional journey stops dead. Francesca is already building bespoke presentation packages. Chuck and James aren't. The fix is systemising what Francesca does across all three reps and threading discovery language into every estimate.

Minimal effort High impact
2

Discovery covers the technical. Not the commercial.

Your team asks the right questions to spec a project. The emotional and commercial questions happen by instinct, or they don't happen at all. Why this. Why now. Who hosts. How do they want guests to feel when they walk into the garden. Documenting the areas to cover doesn't mean scripts. It means consistency across all three reps and a way to measure whether those conversations are happening.

"I encourage the team to ask those questions, but I don't document it, and I don't track it."Miles Hartwell
Minimal effort High impact
3

Qualification sends a price list before value is built

First thing a new enquiry gets is a price list. It qualifies fast. But it frames the entire conversation around cost before anyone has talked about what this will mean to them. Miles, you were already questioning this yourself. Your instinct is right. The qualification can still happen early. The sequence just needs adjusting so value leads. A £150K slide is expensive. A £150K sculpture that transforms how you live is a different conversation entirely.

Minimal effort High impact
Medium Behaviour Change
4

Next steps are client-driven. On a 6-18 month cycle.

Matt confirmed it: next steps happen when the client drives them. Your buyers assign half a day a month to their project. Waiting for them to drive the next conversation is handing over control of your pipeline. The fix is engineering plausible, value-based reasons to schedule the next call. Not pressure. Value. "We've done some further work on your design. When's a good time to walk you through it?" Pipeline velocity and information quality both improve. You stop sitting in the dark wondering where things stand.

Medium effort Very high impact
5

No objective way to measure what's actually happening

This is the smoking gun. Three salespeople. Wild revenue swings quarter to quarter. A deal size where one bluebird makes someone look like a top performer for six months. You don't know if the gap between reps is discovery quality, emotional connection, follow-up cadence, or next steps. You don't know what's luck and what's skill. You're managing a £3.75M pipeline on gut feel.

This doesn't require recording every call. The CRM stages get mandatory fields populated as deals progress. Reps are prompted to capture decision progress, timeline expectations, reasons to buy, and emotional drivers at each gate. Next steps are labelled. The best calls get recorded automatically through a note-taker that sits in the background. The framing to the client: "We record so nothing's lost in translation to the technical team." Optional on any call. The real measurement comes from the CRM gates, not surveillance.

Built into programme Very high impact
6

Enquiry follow-up can extend across channels

Your FUP sequence works for what it does. It brings back unresponsive new enquiries with a clear cadence. Miles built it, and it reflects the philosophy you've instilled across the team: always give something, never just check in. The opportunity is extending that thinking into text and WhatsApp for engaged prospects over longer cycles. "Hey, noticed you'd submitted an enquiry for one of our slides. Would it help if you could see how it would look in your property?" Curiosity. Value. Your philosophy, new channels.

Minimal effort Moderate impact
